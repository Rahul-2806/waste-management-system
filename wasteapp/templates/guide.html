Composting 101: A Beginner's Guide

Transform Your Kitchen Scraps and Yard Waste into Gardening Gold

Introduction to Composting

Composting is nature's way of recycling organic material into a rich, soil-like substance that gardeners affectionately call "black gold." This natural process transforms kitchen scraps, yard waste, and other organic materials into a nutrient-rich fertilizer that can enhance your garden's health while reducing waste sent to landfills.

Whether you have a sprawling garden, a modest yard, or just a small balcony, there's a composting method that will work for your space and lifestyle. This guide will walk you through everything you need to know to start your composting journey.

Did you know? Up to 30% of what we throw away could be composted instead of sent to landfills. Composting at home can significantly reduce your household waste while creating a valuable resource for your garden.

Choosing Your Composting Method

There are several ways to compost, each with its own advantages. The best method for you depends on your available space, the amount of compostable material you generate, and how much time you want to invest in the process.

Traditional Pile or Bin

Best for: Those with yard space who generate a moderate to large amount of yard waste.

Space needed: At least 3' x 3' area in your yard

Time investment: Low to moderate (turning every few weeks)

Time to finished compost: 3-12 months, depending on management

An open pile or simple bin allows for easy addition of materials and turning. This method works well for yard waste and is the easiest to maintain, though it may take longer to produce finished compost.

Tumbler

Best for: Urban or suburban homes with limited space and mainly kitchen scraps.

Space needed: Small footprint, about 2' x 2'

Time investment: Low (turning the tumbler a few times per week)

Time to finished compost: 4-8 weeks with proper management

A sealed drum that you can rotate to mix the contents. Tumblers keep pests out, control odors, and speed up the composting process through frequent turning. They're great for smaller spaces and produce compost relatively quickly.

Vermicomposting (Worm Bin)

Best for: Apartment dwellers or those with minimal outdoor space.

Space needed: Very small, can fit under a sink

Time investment: Low (feeding worms weekly)

Time to finished compost: 2-3 months for first harvest, then continuous

Uses special composting worms (red wigglers) to break down food scraps. Can be done indoors in a small bin, making it perfect for apartments. Produces both worm castings (a very rich fertilizer) and liquid "worm tea."

Bokashi

Best for: Those wanting to compost all food waste, including meat and dairy.

Space needed: Very small, fits in a kitchen

Time investment: Low (adding bokashi bran and draining liquid)

Time to finished compost: 2 weeks fermenting, then 2-4 weeks buried in soil

An anaerobic fermentation process using special microbes. Can process all food waste including meat, dairy, and citrus. Creates a fermented pre-compost that must be buried to complete the process.

What Can You Compost?

A successful compost pile needs a good balance of "green" nitrogen-rich materials and "brown" carbon-rich materials. Aim for a ratio of roughly 1 part green to 3 parts brown materials by volume.

Green Materials (Nitrogen-Rich)

  • Fruit and vegetable scraps
  • Coffee grounds and filters
  • Tea bags (remove staples)
  • Fresh grass clippings
  • Plant trimmings
  • Egg shells (crushed)
  • Herbivore animal manure (horse, cow, rabbit, chicken)
  • Seaweed or kelp
  • Hair and fur
  • Houseplant trimmings

Brown Materials (Carbon-Rich)

  • Dry leaves
  • Small twigs and branches (broken up)
  • Straw
  • Sawdust (from untreated wood)
  • Shredded newspaper or plain paper
  • Cardboard (torn up)
  • Paper egg cartons
  • Toilet paper and paper towel rolls
  • Dried grass
  • Pine needles (in moderation)
  • Nut shells (except walnut)
  • Corn cobs (broken up)

Avoid Composting These Items

  • Meat, fish, and poultry scraps (attracts pests)
  • Dairy products (attracts pests)
  • Oils and fats (slow decomposition, attract pests)
  • Diseased plants (can spread disease)
  • Weeds that have gone to seed
  • Pet waste from dogs and cats (may contain pathogens)
  • Citrus peels and onions in large quantities (can kill worms in vermicomposting)
  • Pressure-treated or painted wood
  • Glossy or coated paper
  • Synthetic fertilizer (can kill beneficial microorganisms)
  • Coal or charcoal ash (may contain substances harmful to plants)
  • Walnut shells (contain juglone, harmful to some plants)
Tip: Keep a small countertop bin in your kitchen to collect food scraps throughout the day. Empty it into your compost pile or bin every few days to prevent odors.

How to Start Composting: Step-by-Step Guide

Choose Your Location

For outdoor composting, select a dry, shady spot near a water source and preferably out of sight. Ensure it's convenient enough that you'll actually use it. The ideal location has good drainage and is at least 2 feet away from structures to allow for air circulation.

For indoor methods like vermicomposting, choose a location that's easily accessible but out of the way‚Äîunder sinks, in utility rooms, or in a garage are all good options.

Gather Your Materials

For a traditional pile or bin:

  • A bin (purchased or DIY) or dedicated space for an open pile
  • A pitchfork or compost turner for mixing
  • A collection container for kitchen scraps

For vermicomposting:

  • A worm bin (purchased or DIY)
  • Composting worms (red wigglers)
  • Bedding material (shredded newspaper, cardboard)

Set Up Your System

Traditional pile or bin: Start with a layer of coarse materials like small branches or straw at the bottom for drainage. Then add alternating layers of brown and green materials.

Vermicomposting: Fill your bin with moistened bedding material, add your worms, and let them settle in for a day before adding food scraps.

Tumbler: Add a mix of brown and green materials until the tumbler is about 3/4 full, then add a compost starter if desired.

Maintain Proper Moisture

Your compost should feel like a wrung-out sponge‚Äîdamp but not soggy. If it's too dry, add water or more green materials. If it's too wet, add brown materials and turn the pile to incorporate more air.

Turn Your Compost

Mixing or turning your compost introduces oxygen, which speeds decomposition and prevents odors. For traditional piles, aim to turn it every 1-2 weeks. For tumblers, give them a few spins every few days.

Vermicomposting systems don't require turning‚Äîthe worms do the work for you!

Monitor and Adjust

Keep an eye on your compost's progress and make adjustments as needed:

  • If it smells rotten, it's likely too wet or has too many green materials
  • If it's not breaking down, it may be too dry or need more green materials
  • If it's attracting pests, make sure you're not adding problematic materials and that the pile is properly covered

Harvest Your Compost

Your compost is ready when it's dark brown, crumbly, and smells like rich earth. Depending on your method and conditions, this can take anywhere from 2 months to a year.

For traditional piles, you can harvest from the bottom where decomposition is complete. For tumblers, stop adding new materials for a few weeks before harvesting. For worm bins, move finished compost to one side and add fresh bedding to the other side; the worms will migrate to the fresh side.

Use Your Compost

There are many ways to use your finished compost:

  • Mix into garden soil before planting
  • Spread as a 1-2 inch top dressing around plants
  • Brew compost tea for a liquid fertilizer
  • Mix with potting soil for containers (about 25% compost)
  • Use as a mulch to retain moisture and suppress weeds

Troubleshooting Common Composting Problems

Problem Possible Causes Solutions
Foul odor (rotten eggs) Too wet, compacted, or too many green materials Add brown materials, turn the pile to aerate, ensure proper drainage
Pile isn't heating up Too small, too dry, not enough green materials, or cold weather Add more materials to increase pile size, add water if dry, add green materials for nitrogen
Attracting pests Meat/dairy products in pile, food scraps exposed at surface Remove problem materials, bury food scraps in the center, use a rodent-proof bin
Decomposition is too slow Pieces too large, pile too dry, not enough nitrogen Chop materials into smaller pieces, add water, add more green materials
Flies or insects Food scraps exposed on surface Bury food scraps in the center, cover with brown materials
Pile is wet and warm in center but nowhere else Pile is too small Add more materials to achieve 3' x 3' x 3' minimum size
Worms dying in vermicompost Too wet/dry, wrong foods, bin too hot/cold Adjust moisture, avoid citrus/onions, keep bin between 55-77¬∞F

Ready to Get Started?

Composting doesn't have to be complicated. Start small, learn as you go, and enjoy the process of creating your own nutrient-rich soil amendment while reducing waste. Your garden‚Äîand the planet‚Äîwill thank you!

Seasonal Composting Tips

Spring

  • Use finished compost in garden beds as you prepare for planting
  • Add yard waste from spring cleanup to start a fresh compost pile
  • Turn overwintered compost to reactivate decomposition
  • Start a new batch in a tumbler for summer use

Summer

  • Monitor moisture levels closely and water if needed during hot weather
  • Add plenty of brown materials to balance out abundant green materials
  • Consider covering the pile during heavy rainstorms to prevent waterlogging
  • Harvest compost for mid-season garden applications

Fall

  • Collect and shred fallen leaves‚Äîthey're perfect brown materials
  • Store extra leaves in bags or bins to use throughout the year
  • Add end-of-season garden waste, being careful to exclude diseased plants
  • Consider insulating outdoor compost bins for continued winter activity

Winter

  • Continue adding kitchen scraps even if decomposition slows
  • Add materials to the center of the pile where it's warmest
  • Consider moving vermicomposting indoors if temperatures drop below 55¬∞F
  • Use a closed bin or insulate an open pile to retain some heat

Conclusion: Your Composting Journey

Composting is both a science and an art. As you gain experience, you'll develop an intuitive sense for what your compost needs and how to maintain the perfect balance for efficient decomposition. Don't be afraid to experiment and adjust your approach based on your specific conditions and needs.

Remember that every bit of organic material you compost rather than send to a landfill makes a positive impact on the environment. Even a small composting operation diverts waste from landfills, reduces methane emissions, and creates a valuable resource for your garden or houseplants.

Start with whatever system fits your space and lifestyle, and you can always expand or modify as you become more comfortable with the process. The most important step is simply to begin.

Happy composting!
